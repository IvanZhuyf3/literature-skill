# Significant enhancement of photoconductivity in truly two-component and chemically hybridized CdS-poly„N-vinylcarbazole… nanocomposites

C. L. Yang, J. N. Wang,a) and W. K. Ge

Department of Physics, Hong Kong University of Science and Technology, Clear Water Bay, Hong Kong, China

S. H. Wang, J. X. Cheng, X. Y. Li, Y. J. Yan, and S. H. Yang

Department of Chemistry, Hong Kong University of Science and Technology, Clear Water Bay, Hong Kong, China

\~Received 25 August 2000; accepted for publication 13 December 2000!

Chemically hybridized CdS-poly\~N-vinylcarbazole! \~PVK! nanocomposites with different molar ratios of CdS to PVK were prepared. A variety of techniques including optical absorption, photoluminescence \~PL!, photocurrent spectroscopy, and time-resolved PL were employed to investigate the properties of CdS–PVK nanocomposites. A significant enhancement of photoconductivity was observed in CdS–PVK nanocomposites as compared to pure PVK and CdS/PVK nanoblends. We interpret this result as the improved interface quality between CdS and PVK in CdS–PVK nanocomposites, which facilitates fast interfacial carrier transfer and thus increases the charge generation efficiency. © 2001 American Institute of Physics.

@DOI: 10.1063/1.1345826#

Photoconductivity enhancement of polymer has been demonstrated when CdS and other semiconductor nanoparticles were finely dispersed in the polymer matrix.1–4 The enhanced photoconductivity is attributed to the carrier transfer effect between the dopants and polymer.5 Therefore the interface structure between the polymer and the semiconductor nanoparticles has a crucial effect on the photoconductivity enhancement since it controls the carrier transfer mechanism and efficiency. So far, the reported samples are prepared by simply mixing the synthesized nanoparticles and polymer. The effect of the inevitably introduced precursor molecules or the capping molecules of the synthesized semiconductor nanoparticles is not clear yet. Furthermore, the conventionally synthesized semiconductor nanoparticles have a tendency to aggregate into larger clusters and the fine dispersion of them in the polymer is not very easy. Recently we have taken a new approach for the preparation of truly two-component CdS–poly\~N-vinylcarbazole \~PVK! nanocomposite.6–8 By sulfonation of PVK, CdS nanoparticles were directly produced in the PVK matrix and CdS–PVK nanocomposites were formed. In this letter, we report the optical and electrical properties of the CdS–PVK nanocomposites. It is shown that the new nanocomposite has a significant photoconductivity enhancement as compared to pure PVK and CdS/PVK nanoblends. This enhancement is attributed to the fast interfacial carrier transfer and thus increases the charge generation efficiency in CdS–PVK nanocomposites.

The chemically hybridized CdS–PVK nanocomposite samples were prepared by: \~1! the sulfonation of PVK, \~2! the preparation of the precursor $\mathrm { P V K } ( \mathrm { S O } _ { 3 } ) _ { 2 } \mathrm { C d }$ , and \~3! the formation of CdS–PVK nanocomposite. Detailed synthesis procedure can be found elsewhere.6 By controlling the sulfonation partial of the PVK, we can successfully vary the molar ratio of CdS to PVK. In this study, two samples named as PVK–10-CdS and PVK–15-CdS were investigated with the molar ratio of CdS to PVK being 1:80 and 1:34, respectively. For comparison, pure PVK, sodium bis\~2-ethylhexyl! sulfocuccinate \~AOT! capped pure CdS nanoparticles, and nanoblends of CdS/PVK samples were also prepared, in which the molar ratio of CdS to PVK for nanoblends of CdS/PVK was kept the same as that for PVK–10-CdS. Ultraviolet-visible \~UV-Vis! absorption spectra were obtained using a Milton Roy 300 spectrometer. Photoluminescence \~PL! spectra were recorded using either a He–Cd laser \~325 nm! or frequency doubled Ti:sapphire laser \~400 nm! as excitation sources. A streak camera was employed in the time resolved PL measurements. In photoconductivity and photocurrent spectroscopic measurements, the thin film samples were fabricated by spin coating nanocomposites or nanoblends onto indium–tin–oxide coated glass. Then aluminum was evaporated on the top to make the upper electrode. The film thickness determined by Alpha stepper is about 120 nm. The active area of the sample is about 0.2 cm2 . A 150 W Xe lamp was used as the source of white light, which passed through a Spex1681 manochromator and was chopped at a frequency of 400 Hz before being focused onto the sample surface. A SR830 lock-in amplifier was used to measure the current between the two electrodes of the sample.

The UV-Vis absorption spectra and PL spectra of pure PVK, pure CdS nanoparticles, nanoblends of CdS/PVK, and the chemically hybridized CdS–PVK nanocomposites are shown in Fig. 1. It is clear from absorption spectra in Fig. 1\~a! that the pure PVK has little absorption beyond 370 nm, while for pure CdS nanoparticles the absorption onset is extended to 440 nm. This onset is determined by plotting $( \alpha h \nu ) ^ { 2 }$ against h and extrapolating $( \alpha h \nu ) ^ { 2 }$ to zero \~ is the absorption coefficient and h is the photon energy!. In addition, a small absorption peak at about 570 nm in the absorption spectrum of pure CdS nanoparticles is observed and attributed to the trap states within the band gap. An average particle size of 3.0 nm for a pure CdS nanoparticles sample is estimated from the band gap absorption onset using the equation in Ref. 9 based on the effective mass approximation for the semiconductor nanoparticles. As shown in Figs. 1\~b! and 1\~c!, the absorption spectra of the CdS/PVK nanoblends and the chemically hybridized CdS–PVK nanocomposites are basically a superposition of the absorption spectra of pure CdS nanoparticles and pure PVK. For the PVK–10-CdS and PVK–15–CdS sample an absorption onset of 473 and 480 nm, respectively, is obtained. As a result, the corresponding average particle size is estimated to be 3.6 and 3.8 nm, respectively.

The PL spectra shown in Fig. 1 are obtained using 400 nm laser excitation. The PL spectrum of pure PVK exhibits a strong emission peak at about 420 nm, while for pure CdS nanoparticles a broad emission peaked at about 560 nm is observed, which is attributed to the trap states related recombination @see Fig. 1\~a!#. Interestingly, the PL emission from the CdS/PVK nanoblends shows two peaks corresponding to the emission from pure PVK and pure CdS nanoparticles, respectively @see Fig. 1\~b!#. However, for the chemically hybridized CdS–PVK nanocomposite samples PVK–10-CdS and PVK–15-CdS only a single peak is observed at about 480 and 490 nm, respectively @see Fig. 1\~c!#. Both emissions are attributed mainly to the band-to-band transition of CdS nanoparticles in the nanocomposites as their peak energies coincide with the absorption spectra onsets. The broadness of the emission is due to the size distribution of the particles. The emission from the PVK matrix is almost quenched. These results indicate that the CdS nanoparticles synthesized directly in PVK have a better quality than those in nanoblends because the surface passivation effect by PVK in nanocomposites reduces the surface defect states and enhances the band-to-band transition of CdS particles. Furthermore, the quenching of the emission from the PVK matrix in the nanocomposites implies that the CdS particles in the composites are effective carriers trapping centers in comparison with those in the nanoblends. It is worth noting that when using 325 nm laser excitation only the emission from the PVK matrix is observed in the PL spectra of CdS–PVK nanocomposite samples. But the emission intensity of the PVK matrix is greatly reduced in comparison with pure PVK. This also implies that the CdS nanoparticles form an effective carrier trapping center in CdS–PVK nanocomposites.

A large enhancement of photoconductivity is observed for CdS–PVK nanocomposites. For sample PVK–10-CdS it is estimated the gain at 2 V is about 50 A/W. Figure 2 shows the photocurrent responses of the CdS–PVK nanocomposites \~PVK–10-CdS! and the CdS/PVK nanoblends at a voltage bias of 3 V. When the white light turns on/off, the current increases/decreases accordingly. The gain for the PVK–10- CdS nanocomposite is about 55 A/W, while for the PVK CdS nanoblends is about 36 A/W. It indicates that the nanocomposite is a better photoconductor than the PVK/CdS nanoblends. Photoconductivity is the convolution of the carrier generation efficiency and carrier transport mobility in the material. Since a small amount of CdS nanoparticles have little effect on the carrier mobility of the PVK matrix, their main function is the enhancement of the carrier generation efficiency. Carrier generation efficiency depends on the competition between the charge separation into free carriers and other decay channels of the photon induced excitons. To understand the mechanism by which the carrier generation efficiency of the PVK is enhanced in the presence of CdS nanoparticles and to investigate the origin of the photoconductivity enhancement, we have measured the short-circuit photocurrent spectra response of the CdS–PVK nanocomposite @curves \~c! and \~d!#, CdS/PVK nanoblends @curve \~b!#, and pure PVK @curve \~a!#, as shown in Fig. 3. When the molar ratio of CdS/PVK in the nanocomposites is small \~for PVK–10-CdS!, the photocurrent spectrum response is almost the same as pure PVK but the photocurrent is largely enhanced @see curves \~a! and \~c! in Fig. 3#. This indicates that the CdS particles have little contribution in the absorption of the incident light to produce free carriers directly but are mainly responsible for the increased carrier generation efficiency in PVK. When the molar ratio of CdS/PVK is relatively large \~PVK–15-CdS!, a new peak corresponding to the absorption of the CdS particles is observed in the photocurrent spectrum @see curve \~d! in Fig. 3#. For the photocurrent spectrum of the CdS/PVK nanoblends the absorption of CdS is more prominent @see curve \~b! in Fig. 3#. Figure 4 shows the electric field dependences of the photocurrent response at illumination wavelengths indicated for PVK–15-CdS. It shows that the photocurrent is increased nearly exponentially with the electric field when the illumination wavelength is set to correspond to pure PVK absorption at 340 nm \~see solid circle in Fig. 4!. While the illumination wavelength is set to correspond to CdS absorption at 450 nm the photocurrent is almost unchanged with increasing electric field \~see solid square in Fig. 4!. This suggests that photocurrent increase is due mainly to the carrier generation efficiency increase in PVK matrix. CdS particles in CdS–PVK nanocomposites play a role in the charge separation of the photoinduced electron-hole (e – h) pairs \~or excitons! in the PVK matrix rather than to produce free carriers directly as in the case of CdS/PVK nanoblends.4

It is well known that both highest occupied molecular orbital and lowest unoccupied molecular orbital of PVK are higher than that of CdS nanoparticles, i.e., a type-II interface is formed between PVK and CdS. As a result, the photoexcited electrons in PVK can be trapped by CdS particles to enhance the charge separation of photoexcited e – h pairs in the PVK matrix. Thus the lifetime of the carriers in PVK is increased. Due to the electron transfer from PVK to CdS, the lifetime of carriers in CdS nanoparticles is also increased. Figures 5\~a!, 5\~b!, and 5\~c! show the PL decay at 460 and 540 nm for pure CdS nanoparticles, CdS/PVK nanoblends, and PVK–10-CdS, respectively. The lifetime of the carriers in pure CdS nanoparticles is about 0.16 and 0.45 ns for 460 and 540 nm, respectively. For CdS/PVK nanoblends, the lifetime is increased to 1.16 and 0.7 ns for 460 and 540 nm, respectively. This sharp increase of lifetime means that the charge transfer occurs between the PVK and CdS nanoparticles. For CdS–PVK nanocomposites, PVK–10-CdS, the lifetime is further increased to 1.76 and 1.38 ns for 460 and 540 nm, respectively. This implies that the charge transfer efficiency between the PVK and CdS nanoparticles in the composites is enhanced in comparison with that in the blend. It in turn explains the enhancement of photoconductivity in CdS–PVK nanocomposites.

In conclusion, PVK–CdS nanocomposites are successfully obtained by chemical hybridization. Large photoconductivity enhancement is observed for the nanocomposites in comparison with PVK/CdS nanoblends. Steady state and time-resolved PL studies indicate that the CdS nanoparticles in the composites are effective carrier trapping centers. The enhanced trapping effect of CdS nanoparticles results in an increase of the charge generation efficiency and is attributed to the improved interface quality between CdS particles and PVK in the nanocomposites.
