# Synthesis and study of Au(III)–indolizine derivatives: turn-on luminescence by photo-induced controlled release†

Jie Yang,a Yifan Zhu,a Anfernee Kai-Wing Tse,b Xinghua Zhou,a Yang Chen,a Yu Chung Tse, \*c Keith Man-Chung Wong \*a and Chun-Yu Ho \*a

The photo- and structural properties of a series of Au(III) indolizine complexes were determined. Controlled release of halogenated indolizine derivatives from the corresponding Au(III) complexes was achieved by photoinduced C–X bond formation, which provided turn-on luminescence with an increase in emission intensity of up to 67 times.

Indolizine derivatives and Au(III) complexes are both interesting classes of chemicals because of their fascinating biological1 and photophysical properties,2,3 which lead to potential applications as drugs and organic sensitizers. Cycloisomerization4,5 of 2-propargylic pyridines, additions, and cross-coupling6–9 are the most common pathways to synthesize organic indolizines.10 However, no Au(III) indolizinyl complexes have been reported to date.11 The protons of simple organic indolizinium salts are generally less acidic than those of most imidazolium salts and thus the corresponding complexes cannot be accessed easily by deprotonation in an analogous process to that used to synthesize Au(III) imidazolium complexes. This situation has hampered the study and use of indolizine derivatives as ligands or photosensitizers for Au(III) as well as other metals for a broad range of applications like catalysis,12a–h transmetallation, sensing, cell coloring, photodriven disease therapy, and reducing environment therapy.12j–m

Our study commenced with the synthesis of Au(III) indolizine derivatives using propargyl pyridine 1 (Scheme 2). Intercepting the H shifts or deprotonation step in typical cycloisomerization at the propargylic position could block the transition-metal elimination from the indolizine derivatives. Therefore, a quaternary center was introduced onto 1 in the hope of providing the desired complexes 3 with sufficient stability. To our delight, Au(III) complexes with a general structure of 3 were obtained in one step from 1 and 2 under operationally simple, mild, and general conditions (see the ESI,† S9). Highly substituted 3 with different functional groups (R1 , R2 , and R3 ), such as silyl, aryl, heteroaryl, methoxy, and alkyl, were obtained in good yield. Moreover, salts like 2a and 2c–e were found to be compatible with the conditions (AuCl, AuBr as well as CuCl, and CuBr), giving the corresponding Au(III) complexes 3, and Au(I) and Cu(I) complexes 4. This allowed us to compare their properties as a function of M center, oxidation state, and counter ion later on.

Au(III) indolizine derivatives were isolated and structurally characterized by X-ray crystallography (3ab, 3bb, 3cb, 3fb, and 3bc).15 Perspective views of the complexes are depicted in Fig. 1 and important structural information is tabulated in the ESI,† S19. In general, (a) Au(III) adopts a slightly distorted square planar geometry to avoid unfavorable steric interactions with $\mathbf { R } ^ { 1 } ; ( \mathsf { b } )$ the Me and OH groups tend to sit at equatorial positions; (c) the $\mathbf { A u - C }$ bond distances are indicative of single bond character; (d) the strong trans influence of the indolizinyl ligand is revealed by the long $\mathbf { A u - C l } _ { t r a n s }$ bond length; (e) the use of OMe or OH does not strongly affect the core geometry; (f) the indolizine plane is almost orthogonal to the $\mathbf { A u X } _ { 3 }$ plane.

Several notable aspects of those non-cyclometalated Au(III) complexes were revealed by further analysis. First, the bond angles of the two $\mathbf { C } { - } \mathbf { A } \mathbf { u } \big ( \mathrm { I I I } \big ) { - } \mathbf { C } \mathbf { l } _ { c i s }$ are different (ESI,† S19, entries 1–3). One $\mathrm { C l } _ { c i s }$ was bent towards the indolizinyl core, which gave a relatively small $\mathrm { C l } _ { c i s } { \mathrm { - A u ( m ) - C } }$ angle. As a result, the structure of 3 is less symmetrical than expected because it does not minimize undesired steric interactions. This suggests that there might be a favorable electronic and/or orbital interaction between one $\mathrm { C l } _ { c i s }$ and the indolizine on Au( ) (see below). Second, for ${ \mathrm { O R } } ^ { 3 } = { \mathrm { O M e } }$ or OH (3bb and 3fb), the $\mathrm { C l } _ { c i s }$ bent towards the indolizinyl core is always located on the same face as $\mathrm { O R } ^ { 3 }$ (i.e., the opposite face to Me, see Scheme 2 and Fig. 1). This indicates that the bending of $\mathrm { C l } _ { c i s }$ towards the indolizinyl core was not a result of simple hydrogen bonding between OH and the halogen and was not controlled by the uneven steric hindrance provided by the tertiary alcohol center. Also, both Cl and the bulkier Br complexes of 3 showed the same bending trend (3bb and 3bc, entries 2 and 5), although $\operatorname { B r } _ { c i s }$ was bent more than $\operatorname { C l } _ { c i s } .$ These observations collectively indicate that the origin of the unusual bending of halogens might be related to an electronic effect rather than sterics. Third, the dihedral angles of the indolizinyl plane and aromatic $\mathbf { R } ^ { 1 }$ plane are in the range of $\sim 4 0 ^ { \circ }$ to 551 for these Au( ) complexes. This structural feature resembles those of most N-aryl NHC transition metal catalysts and suggests the potential of these Au(III) complexes in catalyst development,12 especially in view of their chiral alcohol center on the ring. At the same time, it reduced the degree of conjugation between the two p-systems. This structure might be helpful for turn-on luminescence development because such conjugation was found to be important in the corresponding organic dye.2

Controlled release of organic species from transition metal complexes is an important topic in a number of areas. Controlled release is particularly interesting when the species is a fluorophore with potential applications in biological systems and is photoinactive unless a desired stimulus is applied (Scheme 1).16a–c There has been a surge of interest in the development of luminescence detection techniques with high sensitivity and low instrumental needs. Materials with turn-on luminescence ability are attractive for practical applications such as bioimaging/assays, chemosensing, and super-resolution imagin g.16d–k In complexes 3, the strong emission of the indolizine derivative was suppressed and there was a change in the absorbance intensity of 3 with $\mathbf { A u X } _ { 3 }$ upon excitation (ESI,† S20–24 for spectra). We suspected that indolizine luminescent quenching was brought by Au(III). That exact combination was not studied before and we thought that might have potential to display turn-on luminescence. In this regard, the reactivity and photophysical properties of 3 were studied (ESI,† S25). It is also because (1) selective reductive elimination of ${ \bf X } _ { c i s }$ and the indolizine-like core over a normal elimination of ${ \Chi } _ { 2 }$ might be possible under appropriate conditions in view of the structural information obtained from the crystals;17 and (2) the success of such a transformation might allow controlled release of highly emissive indolizine derivatives (Scheme 3).

The indolizinyl–AuBr complex 3bc was subjected to photoinduced reductive elimination conditions similar to those employed for related $\mathbf { N H C } \mathbf { - A u B r } _ { 3 }$ species.18 First, the chemical and photoreactivity of 3bc were examined. The desired brominated indolizine derivative 5bc was released for the first time. The reactivity of 3bc in this elimination process is notable because C–X bond formation in $\mathrm { N H C - A u ( m ) } \mathbf { X } _ { 3 }$ is reported to be challenging. In addition, neither $\mathbf { B r } _ { 2 }$ nor indolizinyl–Au(I)Br 6bc formed (Schemes 1 and 3).

However, complexes with ${ \mathsf { R } } ^ { 1 } = { \mathsf { T } } { \mathsf { M } } { \mathsf { S } }$ or $\mathsf { R } ^ { 3 } = \mathsf { O } M \mathsf { e }$ decomposed upon prolonged excitation, which prohibited direct comparison of the changes in rate and intensity.

The elimination process to give 5 was highly efficient, general, and presented good substrate scope.17 Indeed, our other AuCl3 complexes also showed similar behavior to that of 3bc (e.g., 3bb and 3cb),19,20 giving the corresponding chlorinated indolizines 5 in 492% yield. In contrast, the control experiments that used Au(I) or Cu(I) under otherwise the same conditions did not provide the halogenated indolizines. Instead, the starting complexes were recovered in good yield as expected, which is attributed to their linear geometry and redox potential. From a spectroscopic point of view, the inertness of 4aa, 4ba, and 4ca towards such a transformation is probably caused by their low-lying singlet excited state, as indicated by their low-energy absorption bands at 334–350 nm (ESI,† S22). All complexes 3 were efficiently converted to 5 according to NMR spectroscopy results, so their differences in emission enhancement were attributed to substituent effects, in accordance with the related literature.2

Second, the halogenated indolizine derivatives 5 were found to display tunable photoactivity, showing good potential for use in turn-on luminescence applications (Scheme 3 and ESI,† S25). The elimination process induced an increase in emission intensity by up to 67 times. Both the choice of halogen X and substituents R on the indolizine-like skeleton could affect the magnitude of the increase in emission intensity induced by elimination. In particular, the increase was larger for chlorinated 5bb than the corresponding brominated analog 5bc, and $\mathbf { R } ^ { 1 } = \mathbf { P } \mathbf { h }$ gave larger emission enhancement than 3-thiophene. Similar to related organofluorophores, $\lambda _ { \mathrm { E m } }$ of 5 could be easily tuned from 383 to 538 nm according to the substituents (ESI,† S25, entries 2, 5, 6, 8, 10, and 11). Notably, $\lambda _ { \mathrm { E m } }$ before and after elimination were nearly the same for these entries. This result strongly suggests that the main function of Au(III) here is the emission suppression of the organofluorophore. Such emission suppression was likely a result of an enhanced spin–orbit coupling in complex 3 by the Au(III) heavy atom through the generation of an indolizine derivative non-emissive triplet state. The presence of such a triplet state was supported by the observation of singlet oxygen emission (see the ESI†). Thus, we tentatively believe that the intersystem crossing (ISC) of Au(III) and indolizine derivatives at their excited states, converted the emissive singlet state to a non-emissive dark triplet excited state in the complexes.

The reaction outcomes and photophysical changes depicted in Scheme 3 may be explained as follows. First, one $\mathbf { X } _ { c i s }$ on 3 was bent towards the indolizine derivative by a favorable electronic and/or orbital interaction, as suggested by the crystallographic information. Second, the selective photoinduced elimination of ${ \bf X } _ { c i s }$ and the indolizine derivative was facilitated by the distorted square planar geometry (Fig. 1 and ESI,† S19). Third, controlled release of halogenated indolizine derivative 5 occurred upon excitation. The singlet oxygen emission experiment indicated that the effective ISC pathway between the indolizine derivatives and Au( ) was blocked upon the set off. As a result, the photoluminescence mechanism of the organic dye was restored, the dihedral angle of the indolizine core and aromatic $\mathbf { R } ^ { 1 }$ was less restricted, and turn-on luminescence was achieved

In summary, the reactivity and properties of Au(III) indolizine complexes were studied. Photoinduced reductive elimination of halogenated indolizine derivatives from Au(III) was observed, which enabled tunable turn-on luminescence with an associated increase in emission intensity of up to 67 times. The results were correlated with an observed singlet oxygen emission signal and the unusual molecular structures were compared with those of NHC analogues. We believe that the Au(III) indolizine complexes may have potential in materials and biological applications.22 In terms of synthesis and ligand development, a general method to synthesize Au(III) indolizine derivatives was established. The high yield, broad substrate scope, complex stability, and good functional group compatibility observed here were attributed to the mild reaction conditions (neutral pH, which is different from the pH needed for deprotonation of imidazolium salts in NHCs) and the quaternary stereocenter employed. The obtained structural information showed that the Au(III) indolizine complexes share a number of similarities with NHC complexes,12 which suggests that they may be utilized as catalysts,13,15c,21 especially in asymmetric catalysis by using the possibly chiral alcohol center.12h,i Applications of the Au(III) indolizine complexes along these lines are now being explored.
