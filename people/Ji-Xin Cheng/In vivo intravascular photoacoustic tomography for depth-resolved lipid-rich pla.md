Abstract: With a quasi-collinear imaging catheter and functional sheath material, we successfully implemented intravascular photoacoustic tomography for in vivo imaging of rabbit aorta, with localization and quantification analysis of lipid distribution in arterial walls.

## 1. Introduction

As the leading cause of death over the world, atherosclerosis exhibits as lipid-rich plaque development within the artery wall, which can lead to life-threatening events due to plaque rupture and thrombosis [1]. Intravascular photoacoustic (IVPA) tomography is a recently developed technique to assess the lipid distribution in arterial wall in complement with traditional intravascular ultrasound (IVUS) due to its absorption-based contrast [2]. Although it has been developed fast in the past several years, technical challenges still exist to prevent the application of IVPA for in vivo study.

In this work, we present a highly-sensitive quasi-collinear IVPA catheter with a functional sheath, which enabled in vivo IVPA imaging of rabbit aortas under clinically-relevant conditions. In addition, we assessed the lipid content in human coronary artery ex vivo by localization and quantification.

## 2. Catheter design and performance

A high sensitivity catheter is the key component in IVPA imaging. In order to produce good imaging quality with ample depth to cover the entire arterial wall, we developed a quasi-collinear IVPA catheter for in vivo applications. A

schematic catheter design is depicted in Fig. 1(a). Excitation laser pulses at 1.7 μm [3] were delivered via a multimode fiber and side-fired to the arterial wall by a rod mirror. US transducer was positioned next to the mirror and tilted by $1 0 ^ { \circ }$ for quasi-collinear overlap with optical beam and reduced multiple US reflection from the sheath, which resulted in an imaging depth ranging from 1.4 mm to 4.6 mm (Fig. 1(b)). A polyurethane sheath material was carefully selected to result minimal artifact and transmission loss for both PA and US signals. The diameter of the catheter including sheath was measured to be 1.6 mm.

## 3. IVPA imaging results

New Zealand White rabbits with 8-months age on normal chow diet were employed for in vivo IVPA imaging under clinical relevant conditions, i.e. imaging with protective sheath, in presence of luminal blood and at speeds up to 16 frames per second (fps). Fig. 2(a) shows a cross-sectional imaging result of rabbit aorta at 4 fps, with PA image (I) indicating the lipid content distribution with depth resolution while US image (II) providing the morphological

information of the artery as well as relative position of lipid deposition. The corresponding histology slice validates our observation, further confirming the lean diet of the rabbit.

Ex vivo IVPA imaging of a human coronary artery at 16 fps and 0.5 mm/s pullback was also conducted to validate the performance of our imaging system and evaluate its clinical significance. Fig. 2(b) shows a representative cross-sectional image result, with both lipid-core plaque (7 o’clock between dashed color lines in Fig. 2(b-II)) and perivascular adipose tissue. The histology again confirmed our observation with thicken intima and plaque indicated by blue arrows. The lipid core depth and its angular ratio, i.e. angle of view over 360°, along the 40-mm-long pullback was derived (Fig. 2(c)), which shows lipid content mainly distributed within a depth range of 1-3 mm from the catheter center. The lipid area in each frame was quantitated and plotted along the pullback direction to provide an overall view of lipid level fluctuation along the artery. The orange line denotes the longitudinal position of the frame showing in Fig. 2(b).

## 4. Conclusion

In conclusion, we presented a quasi-collinear catheter design with a functional sheath material and demonstrated the first in vivo IVPA imaging of rabbit aortas under clinically-relevant conditions. The validation of our imaging system on human coronary artery imaging ex vivo indicates its potential capability for lipid localization and quantification in clinical applications.
