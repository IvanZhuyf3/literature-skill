## 1. Introduction

Nonlinear optical (NLO) microscopy is becoming a powerful tool for studying ex vivo tissues and live animals. Nonlinear dependence on excitation intensity gives NLO microscopy inherent three dimensional imaging capability without the need for a confocal pinhole. This is particularly advantageous in the case of tissue and in vivo imaging where significant scattering can reduce the signal collection efficiency by confocal detection. Laser scanning facilitates real time imaging of live tissues and animals. Also, NLO microscopy utilizes near IR excitation which provides superior optical penetration into tissues [1].

In order to provide a robust imaging system it is necessary to combine several NLO imaging modalities on the same platform because different NLO imaging methods have their distinctive advantages: two-photon excitation fluorescence (TPEF) can be used to visualize proteins, ions with fluorescent labeling or specific autofluorescent structures; sum-frequency generation (SFG) are selective to non-centrosymmetric molecular assemblies such as collagen fibrils; coherent anti-Stokes Raman scattering (CARS) microscopy [2] is naturally sensitive to lipidenriched structures such as myelin sheath [3] and adipocytes [4]. Our group realized that the two picosecond laser beams for CARS imaging could also been used for electronic SFG imaging [5]. We have developed a multimodalilty NLO imaging system that integrates CARS, SFG, and TPEF on the same microscope platform and demonstrated its applications to nervous system [6, 7], artery and atherosclerotic lesions [8, 9], and mammary tumor [10].

## 2. Experimental Setup

A typical setup for multimodality NLO imaging is shown in Fig. 1. Two tightly synchronized picosecond Ti:sapphire lasers (Mira 900 from Coherent Inc. or Tsunami from Spectra Physics) at $\omega _ { \mathrm { p } }$ and ${ \mathfrak { O } } _ { \mathrm { s } }$ were collinearly combined for $\mathrm { S F G } \left( \mathfrak { O } _ { \mathrm { p } } + \mathfrak { O } _ { \mathrm { s } } \right)$ and $\mathrm { C A R S } ~ ( 2 \omega _ { \mathrm { p } } - \omega _ { \mathrm { s } } )$ imaging. The same laser beams are used for TPEF imaging. The laser beams were directed into a laser scanning microscope (IX71/FV300, Olympus America Inc.) and focused into a sample through a 60X water objective with a numerical aperture (NA) of 1.2. External detection channels were constructed for forward CARS and backward CARS, SFG, or TPEF imaging.

## 3. Multimodality NLO Imaging of Central Nervous System

Multimodality NLO microscopy can be used to map the organization of different structures such as myelinated axons and astrocyte processes in the central nervous system [5]. The myelin sheath is a multilayered membrane which surrounds the axon of neuron cells and facilitates high speed impulse conduction. The astrocyte processes interact with neurons to provide structural and trophic support. These two structures in the central nervous system can be visualized by CARS and SFG, respectively, as shown in Fig. 2A. Parallel myelin sheath (red, CARS) which wraps the axons is surrounded by astrocyte processes (blue, SFG). In addition to these structures, the endothelial cell membranes of the blood vessel give a visible CARS contrast. Thus in a perivascular area, we observed a blood vessel (red in Fig. 2B) surrounded by densely packed astrocyte processes (blue in Fig. 2B) in the spinal cord.

Multimodality NLO microscopy can also be used to investigate the pathological pathways of neurological diseases. A variety of neurological diseases target the myelin sheath resulting in its damage or loss (i.e., demyelination). Development of demyelinating lesions is the hallmark of multiple sclerosis, a terminal disease affecting at least 350,000 people in the United States alone. Recently, CARS microscopy was applied to investigate a widely used demyelination model, lysophosphatidyl choline (Lyso-PtdCho) induced myelin degradation in real time [7]. By combining CARS with TPEF, the $\mathrm { C a } ^ { \sum + }$ distribution in healthy and demyelinating states could be monitored, where the CARS signal from myelin sheath defined border between intra- and extra-axonal spaces. Fig. 2 shows CARS (red) and TPEF (green) imaging of live spinal tissue before (Fig. 2C) and after (Fig. 2D) 1 hr incubation with Lyso-PtdCho. Extensive and acute myelin swelling (red in Fig. 2D) characterized by decrease in CARS signal (see the intensity profile below Fig. 2D) was observed. Following treatment with Lyso-PtdCho, the $\mathrm { C a } ^ { 2 + }$ + concentration in the axons was significantly increased, as seen from the images as well as the intensity profiles of Oregon Green BAPTA-2. Thus, multimodality NLO imaging demonstrates that Lyso-PtdCho not only induces myelin swelling but also causes $\mathrm { C a } ^ { 2 + }$ influx into the axons.

## 4. Multimodality NLO Imaging of Atherosclerotic Lesions.

Most recently, CARS-based multimodality NLO microscopy has also been applied to visualize molecular composition of arterial walls and atherosclerotic lesions [8, 9]. NLO images of an atherosclerotic plaque in a iliac artery is shown in Fig. 3. Based on vibrational signals arising from $\mathrm { C H } _ { 2 ^ { - } }$ -rich membrane lipid bilayer, endothelial cells and smooth muscle cells of the arterial walls can be visualized with CARS [9]. Fibrous protein structures including elastin and collagen can also be visualized with CARS due to the abundance of CH2 bonds at the crosslinking regions [9]. Additionally, foam cells rich in lipid droplets and lipid accumulation in the extracellular matrix of an atheroma are readily observable with CARS microscopy [8]. To distinguish CARS signals arising from lipid versus those arising from protein, SHG imaging of collagen fibrils and TPEF imaging of elastin are employed simultaneously with CARS imaging [8]. Multimodal image acquisition of the same area significantly improves the sensitivity and accuracy in visualizing arterial molecular composition. More importantly, label-free multimodal NLO imaging of atheroma allows identification of areas vulnerable to rupture risks, where there is a significant increase in foam cell accumulation coupled with a drastic reduction in collagen fibrils near arterial lumen [8]. Such compositional imaging capability of CARS and other modalities suggests their powerful potential application to the clinical diagnosis of stages of atherosclerotic lesions.
