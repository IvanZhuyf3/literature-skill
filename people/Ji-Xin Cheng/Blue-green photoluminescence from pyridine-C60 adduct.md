# Blue-green photoluminescence from pyridine- $C_{60}$ adduct

## Ji-xin Cheng, Yan Fang, Qun-jian Huang, Yi-Jing Yan $^{1}$ , Xiao-Yuan Li $^{*}$

Department of Chemistry, The Hong Kong University of Science and Technology, Clear Water Bay, Kowloon, Hong Kong, People's Republic of China

Received 5 July 2000; in final form 18 September 2000

## Abstract

The freshly prepared solution of $C_{60}$ in pyridine has a purple-pinkish color, which turns gradually into a brown color. We report for the first time the observation of a bright blue-green photoluminescence from the brown $C_{60}/pyridine$ solution upon UV excitation. The luminescent species is identified to be the zwitterionic adduct formed between pyridine and $C_{60}$ as revealed by ${}^{1}H-NMR$ , mass spectra, and the solvatochromic effect of the photoluminescence to protic solvent. The fluorescence excitation spectra of the luminescent species and the dependence of the luminescence on excitation wavelength are also reported. $C_{70}$ in pyridine shows similar phenomena. © 2000 Elsevier Science B.V. All rights reserved.

## 1. Introduction

Three dimensional conjugation of the $\pi$ electron system in $C_{60}$ makes it a potentially attractive chromophore and fluorophore. However, underivatized pure $C_{60}$ only fluoresces very weakly in the 620–900 nm region with a quantum yield of $3.2 \times 10^{-4}$ and a lifetime of 1.2 ns that are independent of solvents and excitations [1–3]. Recently, strong photoluminescence and electroluminescence were observed from the derivatized $C_{60}$ [4,5], leading to a successful fabrication of a fullerene-based white light LED [5].

Optical properties of the dissolved $C_{60}$ are affected by the nature of solvents [6–10], some of which form adducts with $C_{60}$ in the presence of light [11,12]. Here, we report for the first time that the purple-pinkish color of a freshly prepared $C_{60}$ /pyridine solution changes gradually to brown color and stabilizes thereafter. Unlike the purple-pinkish $C_{60}$ /pyridine solution, which shows no ‘abnormal’ photoluminescence, the brown $C_{60}$ /pyridine solution emits a bright blue-green photoluminescence centered at ca. 470 nm upon UV excitation. We attribute the observed photoluminescence from the brown $C_{60}$ /pyridine solution to new zwitterionic species formed by the adduction of the solvent pyridine molecule(s) to $C_{60}$ , as revealed by ${}^{1}H-NMR$ and mass spectra, as well as the solvatochromic effect in the presence of protic solvent (vide infra).

## 2. Results and discussion

$C_{60}$ (99.9%) was dissolved in pyridine (99.9%, Fisher Scientific) sonically. The freshly prepared solution exhibits a purple-pinkish color, consistent with the previous report [6]. However, the color of this solution turns gradually from purple-pinkish into brown and stabilizes thereafter. The presence of light, especially UV radiation, speeds up the color change process. The observed color change of $C_{60}$ /pyridine solution can be accounted for by the change of UV-visible absorption spectra shown in Fig. 1 (all UV-vis spectra were measured on a Miton Roy Array 3000 spectrophotometer). The absorption of the freshly prepared purple-pinkish $C_{60}$ /pyridine solution (trace A) is almost identical to that of $C_{60}$ in toluene or n-hexane [13]. However, the absorbance of the brown solution (trace B) increases visibly in the 400–480 nm region, which accounts for the observed color change. The inset of Fig. 1 displays the photoluminescence spectra of the purple-pinkish (trace A) and the brown (trace B) solutions, respectively, with excitation at 406.7 nm (the photoluminescence spectra were acquired on a Spex 1403 double monochromator equipped with a Coherent Innova 400 cw $Kr^{+}$ laser operated at 406.7 nm. An average excitation power of 30 mW was used). As can be seen, the trace A is dominated by the Raman scattering peaks of pyridine solvent, despite the very weak luminescent background caused by the presence of excitation light which induced to a certain degree the photo-assisted reaction between pyridine and $C_{60}$ . However, the brown solution shows an intense and broad photoluminescence centered at ca. 470 nm (trace B in the inset of Fig. 1). The sharp spike on top of the luminescence spectrum (trace B) is the genuine Raman scattering of C–H stretching of pyridine molecules. The intensity of the blue-green luminescence increases with the excitation power. These observations suggest that it is likely that the new species, involving both $C_{60}$ and pyridine solvent, is formed and is responsible for the observed blue-green emission of the brown solution. In order to attribute the origin of the blue-green luminescence, we evaporated the pyridine solvent from the brown solution under vacuum and obtained a brown powder. This brown powder could hardly be dissolved in toluene, carbon disulfide, or ethanol. Using an ultrasonic method, the brown powder forms a brown colloidal solution in toluene, which displays the same blue-green photoluminescence as that observed from the homogeneous brown solution.

We have also observed similar phenomena for the solution of $C_{70}$ in pyridine, namely, the color of the freshly prepared $C_{70}/pyridine$ solution turns gradually from purple-pinkish to brown, and the brown solution displays intense blue-green photoluminescence upon UV excitation (data not shown).

Fig. 2 displays the dependence of the luminescence profile of the brown solution on the excitation wavelength from 350 to 450 nm measured on a Hitachi F-4500 Fluorescence Spectrometer. No shift of the luminescence peak is observed with the variation of excitation wavelength. The luminescence excitation spectrum detected at the emission wavelength of 480 nm shows two peaks at 403 and 421 nm, respectively (Fig. 2 inset), consistent with the prominent increase of the absorbance of the brown solution in the 400–440 nm region (Fig. 1).

The observed color change and the unusual luminescence of the brown $C_{60}$ /pyridine solution indicate that there has occurred a strong interaction or reaction between pyridine and $C_{60}$ . It was reported recently that pyrrolidine molecules undergo stepwise adduction to $C_{60}$ , eventually forming a hexapyrrolidine derivative of $C_{60}$ which emits a bright green luminescence [4]. Second, the ${}^{1}$ H-NMR spectrum of the brown species in CDCl $_{3}$ solvent shows three peaks at $\delta = 8.82$ , 8.45 and 7.96 ppm, respectively, with an intensity ratio of 2:1:2. These peaks are shifted to a higher field compared to the chemical shifts of $\delta = 8.81$ , 7.86 and 7.48 ppm of the pure pyridine dissolved in CDCl $_{3}$ (NMR spectra were recorded on a Bruker ARX 300 MHz spectrometer and calibrated with a chemical shift of 7.26 ppm from CH $_{3}$ Cl). This behavior is similar to that observed in the amine-C $_{60}$ adducts [14]. Third, the photoluminescence of the brown solution exhibits a very sensitive solvatochromic effect to protic solvent, as displayed in Fig. 3. The addition of protic solvent such as ethanol, water, or aqueous HCl to the brown C $_{60}$ /pyridine solution quenches the blue-green luminescence with the efficiency of ethanol < H $_{2}$ O < HCl (aq). This phenomenon is expected for the proposed zwitterionic species. However, from all above evidence, we are reluctant to conclude as to exactly how many pyridine molecules are adducted onto a C $_{60}$ , considering especially that an adduct with multi-pyridine on a C $_{60}$ may not be stable under the conditions for acquiring mass spectrum. Its fragments also contribute to the observed mass peaks at C $_{60}$ (720 m.u.) and Py-C $_{60}$ (799 m.u.). It is very likely that several pyridines, instead of one, are adducted to a C $_{60}$ , similar to that observed in the amine-C $_{60}$ and toluene-C $_{60}$ adducts [11,12,14].

## 3. Conclusions

In summary, we observed a color change from purple-pinkish to brown in the $C_{60}$ /pyridine solution. We showed that the brown solution emits a bright blue-green luminescence upon UV excitation. Both the color change and the blue-green luminescence are attributed to the formation of the zwitterionic adducts between pyridine and $C_{60}$ , as supported by its ${}^{1}H-NMR$ and mass spectra, as well as the strong solvatochromic effect of the photoluminescence in the presence of protic solvent. We also observed similar phenomena for $C_{70}$ /pyridine solution. Further study of the interaction and reaction between fullerenes and pyridine may offer a new approach for the functionalization of fullerenes [15,16] and the fabrication of novel fullerene-based luminescent materials [5].
