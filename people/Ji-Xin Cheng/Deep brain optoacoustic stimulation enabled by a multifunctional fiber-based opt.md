# Deep Brain Optoacoustic Stimulation Enabled by a Multifunctional Fiber-based Optoacoustic Emitter

Nan Zheng1 , Ying Jiang2,3, Shan Jiang6 , Yueming Li4 , Xiaoting Jia6 , Chen Yang1,6 and Ji-Xin Cheng1,3,5\* 1 Division of Materials Science and Engineering, 2 Graduate Program for Neuroscience, 3 Photonics Center, Boston University, 4 Department of Mechanical Engineering, 5 Department of Biomedical Engineering, Boston University, 6 Department of Chemistry, Boston, MA 02215, USA. 7 Bradley Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering, Virginia Tech, Blacksburg, VA, USA. Author e-mail address: jxcheng@bu.edu

Abstract: Optoacoustic neurostimulation is a non-genetic light-mediated technique allows high precise control of neural activities. Here, we combined optoacoustic stimulation and electrophysiological recording to achieve bidirectional deep brain stimulation.

## 1. Introduction

Deep brain stimulation (DBS) is a technique allows neuromodulation in a target circuit or anatomical structure of brain. Initially, DBS was found to be a life-changing therapy for motor disorders, like essential tremors and Parkinson disease (PD) and since then, the technique has opened a new frontier for brain diseases treatment[1]. To date, its function has been demonstrated in the treatment of more and more brain diseases, including epilepsy, pain management, dystonia and also under active investigation for other conditions linked to brain pathological circuit, including major depressive disorders and Alzheimer disease[2]. Modern DBS systems are similar to cardiac pacemaker. Stimulation current is generated by the pulse generator and delivered to the target through the implanted electrode. DBS system have slowly evolved in the past two decades. Advances in the understanding of brain disorders and breakthrough in neural engineering, such as electrode design and closed-loop control, are expected to improve the efficacy and tolerability. However, the intrinsic property of electrical stimulation limits its potential for further enhancement. For example, the current leakage during electrical stimulation not only reduce the spatial resolution but also generate strong artifacts in real-time recording[3], which is the key feedback for closed-loop stimulation[4]. Although optogenetics has been demonstrated as a successful strategy for the gaps, the requirement of genetical encoding narrows the application scenarios of those powerful tools and especially obstacles its translation from bench to patients.

Optoacoustic neural modulation is an emerging non-genetic light-mediated technique allows temporally and spatially precise control of neural activity. The optoacoustic process is initiated by delivering a pulsed laser on the absorber, and then broadband acoustic waves will be generated by the transient heating and thermal expansion. Compared with electrical stimulation, our previous work achieved single neuron spatial resolution using a tapered fiber-based optoacoustic emitter[5]. As a light mediated acoustic stimulation method, the interference between optoacoustic stimulation and electrophysiological recording is minimal, which is desired for closed-loop stimulation. In this work, we developed a bidirectional neural interface allowing simultaneous optoacoustic simulation and electrophysiological recording in deep brain. We demonstrated its spatial confined stimulation function in cultured rat cortical neurons by calcium imaging and also its ability for simultaneous optoacoustic stimulation and extracellular recording in mouse hippocampus.

## 2. Results and Discussions

To enable simultaneous optoacoustic stimulation and electrophysiological recording, we developed a multifunctional fiber-based optoacoustic emitter (mFOE). The multifunctional fiber consists of four BiSn metal alloy electrodes and a central optical waveguide using polycarbonate (PC, refractive index nPC = 1.586) and polyvinylidene difluoride (PVDF, refractive index nPVDF = 1.426) as the core and the shell, as previously reported[6]. An optoacoustic conversion layer (10% carbon black in Polydimethylsiloxane, PDMS) was deposited on the central optical waveguide using the pico-liter injector (PLI-100A, Harvard apparatus) as shown in Fig. 1a. To investigate whether the mFOE can directly modulate neural activities, we first examined the response of cultured rat cortical neurons under the stimulation. Because calcium ions enter the neurons during action potential firings, we treated rat cortical neurons (days in vitro 10 to 14) with a calcium indicator, Oregon Green™ 488 BAPTA-1 dextran (OGD-1), and performed calcium imaging (Fig. 1b) using an inverted wide-field fluorescence microscope. The laser power was 70 mW with repetition rate of 1.7 kHz and the duration was 100 ms. Neurons within the emitter area (dash-dot circle in Fig. 1b) showed calcium transients with a success rate (a successful

stimulation was assumed when the coincidence $\Delta \mathrm { F } / \mathrm { F } _ { 0 }$ is larger than 2%) of 80%, while neurons outside the emitter area shown less or no calcium changes with a success rate of 20%. That confirmed the spatial confinement of mFOE stimulation. In addition, we also investigated the ability of mFOE for simultaneous optoacoustic stimulation and electrophysiological recording in vivo. mFOE was implanted into the hippocampus of wild type adult mice (C57BL/6, aged between 56-70 days). With increasing laser duration, stronger neural responses were observed in the local field potential (LFP) recorded by the electrode from mFOE without noticeable artifacts.

## 3. Conclusion

We developed a multifunctional fiber-based optoacoustic emitter as the bidirectional neural interface for deep brain stimulation. A selective deposition method was used to integrate the optoacoustic converter into the fiber. The spatial confined optoacoustic stimulation was demonstrated in cultured rat cortical neurons using calcium imaging. In addition, the ability of mFOE for simultaneous deep brain optoacoustic stimulation and electrophysiological recording was confirmed in mouse hippocampus. Paved the foundation for future closed-loop optoacoustic deep brain stimulation.
